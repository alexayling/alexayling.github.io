I am a Ph.D. candidate at the California Institute of Technology, and I am advised by Professor Ali Hajimiri. My research focuses on high-speed analog IC design, mm-wave and microwave RFIC design, phased arrays, and wireless systems. I have led multiple successful chip tape-outs in 22 nm CMOS FDSOI, 28nm CMOS, 65nm CMOS, and 130nm SiGe BiCMOS, and have experience designing and testing integrated circuits blocks such as phase shifters, frequency multipliers, mixers, amplifiers, power amplifiers, high-speed LO distribution, high-speed ESD protection, biasing, and custom digital blocks.

My research has contributed to space-based solar power capabilities, including the first demonstration of wireless power transmission in space (Caltech News Release). Prior to Caltech, I completed a Masters in Electrical Engineering from the University of Illinois, Urbana-Champaign working with Professor Elyse Rosenbaum on ESD Integrated Circuit Reliability. I received a Bachelors degree in Electrical Engineering from the University of Minnesota, Twin-Cities in 2018. I was a High-Speed/mm-Wave ASIC Research intern at Nokia Bell Labs in 2025 and an ESD Reliability Research intern at Analog Devices Inc in 2019.

  3. Wireless power transfer in space using flexible, lightweight, coherent arrays
    Alex Ayling, Austin Fikes, Oren S. Mizrahi, and 10 more authors
    Acta Astronautica, 2024
    Abs DOI

    Space solar power (SSP), envisioned for decades as a solution for continuous, stable, and dynamically dispatchable clean energy, has seen tremendous interest and a number of experimental demonstrations in the last few years. A practical implementation has been elusive to date, owing to the high launch costs associated with heavy, rigid photovoltaic and wireless power transfer (WPT) arrays. Lightweight and flexible solutions for WPT have been demonstrated terrestrially but, to date, have not been deployed and tested in space. In this paper, we present an experimental space demonstration of a lightweight, flexible WPT array powered by custom radio-frequency integrated circuits. The transmit arrays, receive arrays, and the rest of the system were operated and tested for eight months in Low Earth Orbit (LEO). Results from these experiments, including pointing of the array’s beam to Earth and its detection by a ground station, are presented and discussed in detail. Observations and results from this mission uncover existing strengths and weaknesses that inform future steps towards realizing SSP.
